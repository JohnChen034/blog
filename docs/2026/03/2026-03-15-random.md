In genome seqencing, the problem is we see too much and dont know which is important. So the problem lies in interpretation and prioritization, which is what we do. They try to figure out what kinds of variant information helps prioritize ranking. Currently, we have this guideline:

1. Is it rare in population
2. in silico prediction if this variation is damaging. 
3. similarity to patient's phenotype
4. family inheritance. 
5. existing disease report or functional evidence.

## Slide 1 — Variant deleteriousness prediction

### 1. Consequence type

* Protein-truncating variants: stop-gained, frameshift
* Canonical splice-site variants
* Missense variants
* Synonymous variants
* Intronic variants
* UTR variants
* Nearby splice-region and noncoding regulatory variants

### 2. Missense pathogenicity predictors

* REVEL
* MVP
* Evolutionary Action
* Other missense effect scores (for prioritizing damaging amino acid substitutions)

### 3. Splice prediction

* SpliceAI
* Predicted donor or acceptor loss
* Predicted cryptic splice-site gain
* Predicted exon skipping or transcript disruption
* Useful for near-splice and deep intronic variants

### 4. Clinical pathogenicity label

* ClinVar pathogenic / likely pathogenic
* Variant of uncertain significance (VUS)
* Likely benign / benign
* Conflicting interpretations
* Review status / level of supporting clinical evidence

---

## Slide 2 — Family segregation / inheritance

* **De novo variants**

  * Present in the proband but absent in both parents
  * Often highly informative for severe early-onset disorders

* **Autosomal dominant inheritance**

  * One heterozygous pathogenic variant may be sufficient
  * Should be interpreted together with parental status and known disease mechanism

* **Autosomal recessive inheritance**

  * Two pathogenic variants in the same gene
  * Homozygous or compound heterozygous models
  * Phase matters: ideally consistent with variants being in trans

* **Segregation across family members**

  * Whether the variant co-segregates with disease status
  * Whether unaffected relatives also carry the same candidate variant

* **Unaffected parent / incomplete penetrance**

  * A variant inherited from an unaffected parent may weaken support
  * Still possible under incomplete penetrance or variable expressivity

* **Sex-limited or sex-influenced expression**

  * A transmitted dominant variant may still be plausible if expression is sex-limited

* **Inheritance-model compatibility**

  * Candidate variants should be evaluated under the most plausible Mendelian model for the case

---

## Slide 3 — Relevance to phenotype

### 1. Gene–phenotype match

* Whether the candidate gene is linked to the patient’s core HPO terms
* Overlap between known gene-associated features and the patient’s presentation
* Strength of prior gene-level disease association

### 2. Disease-level similarity

* Similarity between the patient phenotype and known disease phenotypes
* Matching to disease entities associated with the candidate gene
* Support from disease databases and phenotype annotations

### 3. Similar patients

* Previously reported patients with overlapping phenotypes
* Patients with the same causal gene
* Patients with a similar clinical presentation, even if the exact diagnosis differs

### 4. Phenotype expansion openness

* Allowing broader or partially overlapping presentations
* Accounting for variable expressivity
* Not requiring an exact match to the currently known phenotype spectrum

### 5. Phenotype-informed prioritization

* Integrating molecular evidence with phenotype similarity
* Prioritizing variants in genes that better explain the full clinical picture
